Mailchimp Review 2025

★4.3/5(308 reviews)
Free up to 500 contacts

The OG email platform. Easy to use, scales with you, and has everything from newsletters to automation.

Andy's Take

The UI is friendly, the free tier is generous (500 contacts), but you'll outgrow it fast. Pricing jumps sharply at 10K+ contacts. Automation is weak compared to ActiveCampaign or ConvertKit.

What It Does

**What it does:** Email marketing platform that lets you build lists, send campaigns, and automate sequences without needing a developer.

**Why it matters:** If you're building an email list (and you should be), you need somewhere to send those emails. Mailchimp makes it stupid simple — drag-and-drop editor, templates that don't look like spam, and automation that actually works.

**Who it's for:** Agencies managing client email lists, consultants building their own audience, or anyone who needs reliable email delivery without the tech headaches.

**Andy's notes:** I used Mailchimp for years before moving to more advanced platforms. It's perfect when you're starting out — free up to 500 contacts, and the interface is so easy your grandma could use it. Once you hit 10K+ contacts or need serious automation, you'll outgrow it. But for most agencies? This does the job.

Andy's Honest Take

Mailchimp is what everyone starts with because it's familiar and has a generous free tier. Then they realize they've outgrown it and migration is painful.

The free plan gives you 500 contacts and 1,000 monthly email sends. For a brand new newsletter or testing email marketing, this is perfect. The interface is friendly, templates are decent, it's hard to break things.

But here's the problem: You grow to 2,000 contacts and suddenly you're paying $40-60/month. Hit 10,000 contacts? Now you're at $200-350/month. Get to 50,000 contacts? $500-800/month. The pricing scales aggressively.

Compare to Brevo (formerly Sendinblue): Unlimited contacts, you only pay for emails sent. Someone with 50,000 contacts but low email frequency saves $500+/month switching from Mailchimp to Brevo.

Where Mailchimp gets you:

The automation is basic. You can build simple welcome sequences and abandoned cart flows, but complex behavioral triggers and conditional logic get messy. If you need advanced automation, ActiveCampaign or ConvertKit will frustrate you less.

The deliverability is good but not great. I've run side-by-side tests with Mailchimp vs ConvertKit vs Brevo. Mailchimp open rates were consistently 5-10% lower. Still acceptable (20-25% open rates), but noticeably worse than competitors.

The form builder is clunky. You can create signup forms, but customization is limited. If you want beautiful, on-brand forms, you'll end up using Typeform or another tool and integrating via Zapier.

The audience segmentation works but feels dated. You can tag and segment contacts, but the interface makes it harder than it should be. ConvertKit's tag-based system is much more intuitive.

Where Mailchimp actually works well:

The template library is huge. Hundreds of pre-designed email templates for every industry. You can have a professional-looking newsletter in 15 minutes.

The drag-and-drop editor is genuinely user-friendly. Non-technical team members can build emails without needing designer help.

The reporting is solid. Open rates, click rates, geographic data, device breakdowns. You get the metrics that matter without information overload.

The integrations are extensive. Thousands of apps connect to Mailchimp. Shopify, WordPress, WooCommerce, Stripe, Salesforce - everything plugs in.

For e-commerce: Mailchimp has strong e-commerce features (product recommendations, abandoned cart automation, purchase tracking). If you're running a Shopify store, Mailchimp is a natural fit.

For agencies: Mailchimp is not great for managing multiple client lists. You need separate accounts for each client, which gets messy and expensive. Tools like Brevo have better multi-client management.

For creators/solopreneurs: Mailchimp is fine for newsletters and basic automation. But if you're building complex funnels or need advanced segmentation, ConvertKit is purpose-built for creators.

The brand name matters: Clients recognize Mailchimp. If you tell a client 'we use Mailchimp for email,' they nod and trust it. If you say 'we use Brevo,' they ask 'what's that?' Sometimes brand recognition is worth paying extra.

Customer support is mediocre. Free tier gets email support only (slow). Paid plans get chat support, but quality is inconsistent. Expect 24-48 hours for real help.

Pricing reality:

Free: 500 contacts, 1,000 emails/month, basic features Essentials: $13/mo (500 contacts), $35/mo (2,500), $60/mo (5,000) Standard: $20/mo (500), $50/mo (2,500), $100/mo (5,000) - includes automation Premium: $350/mo starting (10,000 contacts minimum) - advanced features

The pricing tiers are confusing and you'll end up paying more than expected.

The migration headache: Once you have 5,000+ contacts and 12 months of data in Mailchimp, switching to another platform feels overwhelming. Exporting contacts is easy. Rebuilding automations and segment logic? That's the painful part.

My advice: If you're just starting, use Mailchimp free tier. But once you hit 2,000+ contacts, evaluate Brevo (unlimited contacts, pay per email) or ConvertKit (better for creators, easier automation) before committing to paid Mailchimp plans.
